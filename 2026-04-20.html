Vinyl Culture · Music Industry Analysis
They Cloned Her Voice, Then Claimed Her Songs | AI Music Scams Are Using Copyright Law as a Weapon Against Real Artists
Vinyl Culture documents a structural exploit that flips copyright infrastructure against the artists it was designed to protect. Folk musician Murphy Campbell had her voice cloned, fake songs uploaded to Spotify under her name, and then her original YouTube videos demonetized via Content ID — by the same distributor that uploaded the fakes. The piece names the conflict nobody's saying out loud: the platform running the automated takedown system is the same company pouring billions into the underlying AI. If you've wondered how long before generative audio becomes a weapon rather than a tool, here's your case study.

Beauty Blew a Fuse · Music & Scene
Satie, Budd, AFX, & the Pursuit of Musical Beauty
Mark Richardson — former Pitchfork editor-in-chief, so worth your time — traces a lineage from Satie's Gymnopédies through Harold Budd to Aphex Twin, built around a single structural argument: that silence isn't absence, it's design. The piece is sharpest when it observes that Satie's music was essentially waiting for digital playback to realize itself — no surface noise, nothing extraneous — which makes the Aphex Twin connection feel less like genre history and more like inevitability.
Critical essay / music history
Joel Gouveia · Music Industry Analysis
Stop Celebrating the Live Nation Verdict
Joel Gouveia, writing at The Artist Economy, does the math the celebratory headlines skipped: the Live Nation antitrust verdict found Ticketmaster deliberately overcharged fans $1.72 per ticket over four years — which extrapolates to roughly a billion dollars siphoned from fans to shareholders. His argument is structural: this wasn't predatory pricing, it was normalized extraction with nowhere else to go, and a verdict without breakup doesn't change the architecture of the problem. If you're tempted to call this a win, Gouveia would like you to show your work.

02 Music & Scene Music
Pitchfork
Sixty Years of 'Come Out'
Andy Beta marks the 60th anniversary of Steve Reich's *Come Out* by returning to the brutality that produced it — a Black teenager in 1964 forcing open a wound just to prove he was bleeding. The piece works because it doesn't separate the music from its origin: those 20 words, looped and phased into something formally radical, carry the violence of where they came from. Essential context for anyone who thinks minimalism is just an aesthetic.

The Geese Hype Machine: Marketing and Authenticity in Music
New Environments uses the Geese rollout — heavily engineered by Chaotic Good Projects through coordinated interviews, clips, and hype placement — to probe a question the indie world would rather not answer: does manufactured discovery actually invalidate genuine taste? The piece is sharper than most authenticity-in-marketing hand-wringing because it doesn't moralize. Bass Culture
Lodger in the Dub: How David Bowie's Berlin Period Discovered Reggae's Rhythmic Logic
Bass Culture makes the case that Bowie and Tony Visconti arrived at dub's core logic — treating time as a compositional material — independently and simultaneously with King Tubby in Kingston. The argument is tightest around 'Yassassin,' where Visconti literally coached Dennis Davis into reggae's kick-on-the-backbeat displacement, and it extends outward to connect Visconti's Eventide Harmonizer work on 'Heroes' to what Tubby was doing at Dromilly Avenue. Not a listicle; this is genuine structural analysis of how production philosophy travels across traditions.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Music Business Worldwide
Streaming Fraud Doesn't Respect Silos. Neither Should We.
Merlin CEO Charlie Lexton uses MBW to argue that the streaming fraud problem is fundamentally a coordination failure — and that DSPs catching bad actors after the fact is the wrong model. Writing with some skin in the game (Merlin's TikTok licensing dispute is addressed directly), Lexton puts a concrete number on the scale: $20M in royalties recovered and reallocated over two years. The ask to platforms is specific — share intelligence, catch fraud upstream, stop treating it as a competitive moat.

Infinite Catalog
Louder & Clearer, Part 2: The Biggest Losers, The $1B Shift, and the $1M Ceiling
Infinite Catalog's second deep dive into streaming royalty redistribution argues that Spotify's artist-centric payout shift isn't lifting all boats — it's concentrating gains at the top while creating a hard ceiling for the middle class. The piece quantifies the $1 billion shift and identifies who's actually losing under the new model. The data is granular, the implications are structural, and the $1M ceiling observation deserves attention from anyone building a career on platform economics.

The Honest Broker
Did Streaming Subscription Prices Just Hit the Wall?
Ted Gioia at The Honest Broker argues that streaming's relentless price-hiking cycle has finally met its limit — and the survey data backs him up. Deloitte found 40% of consumers already cut subscriptions in the past three months, and 61% say they'd cancel their favorite service over a $5 increase. Gioia's structural diagnosis is sharp: platforms didn't just raise prices, they trapped users through audience capture and punished non-subscribers with ads, and that exploitation is now producing the backlash.

Don't Be Evil was always too low a bar
Ricky Sutton's Substack opens with a deceptively familiar setting — Buck's of Woodside, the Silicon Valley diner where PayPal got funded and Netscape planned its moves — to make a structural argument about tech's ethical floor. A former Googler, speaking on background while negotiating severance, names the disconnect precisely: genuine empathy outside work, and engagement metrics inside. A brief history of techno-negativity

Brian Merchant's *Blood in the Machine* publishes a guest essay by Thomas Dekeyser — a human geography lecturer at Southampton whose new University of Minnesota Press book traces how communities have refused machines across centuries. 06 AI Product News AI
AI as Normal Technology
Open-world evaluations for measuring frontier AI capabilities
The familiar AI benchmark problem — models acing tests that don't reflect anything real — gets a serious treatment in this 8,000-word collaborative paper from AI as Normal Technology. The argument: saturation on standard benchmarks tells us almost nothing about whether an AI can actually ship something. The authors introduce "open-world evaluations" and a 17-researcher coalition called CRUX that tests exactly this. Their first result is genuinely concrete: an AI agent built and published an iOS app to the App Store with only two errors. The implied warning about AI-driven app store spam is the kind of downstream signal worth paying attention to.

Google Is Negotiating a Deal With the US DOD That Would Let the Pentagon Deploy Gemini AI Models in Classified Settings
The company that staged the most visible AI ethics walkout in Silicon Valley history is now negotiating to put Gemini into classified Pentagon systems. The Information reports Google is in active talks with the DOD to reverse the position it took in 2018 — when employee protests over Project Maven pushed it out of military contracting entirely. It's now competing with Anthropic and Palantir for the same space. The about-face is a clean illustration of how fast the stated ethics of AI labs bend under competitive pressure.

Media & the Machine
Top Podcaster: AI Kills Info Podcasts—Not Personalities
Media & the Machine's Rob Kelly talks with podcaster Chris Hutchins about a distinction worth sitting with: AI doesn't threaten podcasting, it threatens commodity podcasting — the "10 tips" episodes, the interview formats built around information transfer rather than relationship. Hutchins argues that personalized AI-generated audio will eat that format whole, while personality-driven shows built on trust may actually appreciate in value. The flip side he floats — hosts licensing their voice into LLMs as a 24/7 presence — is where this gets directly relevant to how streaming platforms think about talent and format.
