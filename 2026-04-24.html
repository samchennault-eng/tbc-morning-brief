POW MAG · Music & Scene
Who Deserves the Future?
POW MAG takes on the question no one wants to answer cleanly: what do we do with Afrika Bambaataa's legacy now? The piece doesn't offer absolution or erasure — it sits in the discomfort of a foundational figure whose abuse allegations have been credibly documented, asking what it means to honor hip-hop's origins without sanctifying the men who built them. For anyone who's ever had to navigate the gap between an artist's influence and their conduct, this is the version of that conversation that actually engages the stakes.

People vs Algorithms · Music Industry Analysis
Death Stars
People vs Algorithms examines what happens when the platforms that were supposed to democratize music discovery start behaving like gravity wells — pulling everything toward their centers and crushing whatever gets too close. The piece traces how streaming's winner-take-all dynamics have created a landscape where the middle has effectively collapsed, leaving superstars and hobbyists with nothing viable in between. If you've felt the floor dropping out of what used to be a sustainable music career, this maps the structural forces behind that feeling.

Stems · AI + Content & Editorial Technology
AI Tunes Are Flooding Apple Music, and Almost Nobody Is Listening to Them
Stems digs into a revealing data point: AI-generated tracks are flooding Apple Music's catalog, but almost nobody is actually streaming them. The piece surfaces the uncomfortable gap between upload volume and listener demand — a glut of synthetic content that exists but doesn't circulate. For anyone thinking about what catalog inflation actually looks like in practice, this is the snapshot: the pipes are full, but the water isn't flowing. The question becomes whether platforms will treat this as a curation problem or a licensing opportunity.

02 Music & Scene Music
Maximum Exposure Inc.
Prince and His Machines
Maximum Exposure Inc. traces the Electrifying Mojo — the Detroit FM DJ who built a citywide ritual around blinking porch lights and car headlights — as the missing infrastructural link between Black futurist funk and the birth of techno. The argument is familiar in broad strokes (yes, Mojo influenced the Belleville Three), but the piece earns its ground by treating radio programming itself as compositional practice: a nightly act of genre desegregation that made the synthesis possible before anyone had a name for it.

Hip-Hop's Fate Rests On A Fight Between Dreamers and Monsters
The Andre Gee Newsletter reframes hip-hop's current crisis not as a death but as a reckoning — and the argument lands harder than most takes on the same terrain. The piece draws a clean line between fans who want decentralized, independent scenes and those nostalgic for major-label monoculture, then makes the case that the latter is the actual problem. POW MAG
Who Deserves the Future?
POW MAG frames Afrika Bambaataa's death as an occasion to reckon with what hip-hop actually required to exist: not just genius, but stolen equipment, gang infrastructure, and a specific Bronx context that made his influence feel inevitable to everyone watching. The piece uses the 1977 blackout looting as its entry point — foundational figures grabbing their first speakers off the shelf — to argue that resourcefulness wasn't incidental to the music, it was the music. Sharp and specific, and worth the click.
Your note
Bass Culture: Producer's Corner
The Quiet Architect: Ernest Ranglin and the Birth of Jamaican Music
Bass Culture makes the case that one technique — an offbeat guitar upstroke borrowed from New Orleans second-line and shifted to beat two — is the structural hinge on which all Jamaican popular music turns. Ernest Ranglin made that decision in a Kingston studio when the supply of American R&B records dried up and local musicians had to invent a replacement. He was there for ska's first recordings, for Marley's earliest sessions, and for the 1963 'My Boy Lollipop' crossover. The architecture was his; the credit largely went elsewhere.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Infinite Catalog
Louder & Clearer, Part 3: An Old Hope
Infinite Catalog makes the case that user-centric royalties aren't a lost cause — they're an increasingly urgent one. The argument is structural: pro-rata concentrates wealth at the top by design, and their own 2025 data shows .5% of artists capturing nearly half the income. The piece's sharpest move is reframing user-centric not just as a redistribution fix but as a fan engagement play — if listeners knew their subscription dollars actually reached the artists they love, the whole ecosystem gets more purchase.

People vs Algorithms
Death Stars
People vs Algorithms frames the real tension inside a16z's media thesis: the firm managing $90B+ and 123 unicorns has built an entire story engine around creator-first micro-niches becoming the default feed — but the counter-argument is pretty simple. Most people don't want 12-hour pods and Substacks from threadbois. They want ambient, shared culture. Erik Torenberg's "very insular" shot at the a16z worldview names something worth sitting with: offense-floods-the-zone is a founder posture, not a description of how most humans actually consume media.
Your note
Joel Gouveia
The Death of the Record Deal
The old music industry playbook — vans, club circuits, regional radio, A&R execs with promises — turns out to be optional. Drake, The Weeknd, and Alessia Cara all became household names before they ever played a live show, building audience online first and letting the stage catch up later. The piece uses that pattern to ask the uncomfortable question: if empire-level careers are now built without labels, what were labels actually for in the first place?

Building Blocks
A (working) framework for building collective power in music
Building Blocks picks up a thread from a Music Business Worldwide op-ed arguing artists need to unionize to gain real leverage over where the industry is heading — and immediately hits the practical wall: most unions and relevant policies are domestic, while the music industry is global. In the US, artists can't even legally organize without new legislation first. The Protect Working Musicians Act was one attempt; it stalled. The piece is useful for laying out why the gap between 'artists should have collective power' and 'here's how they get it' remains so wide.

Nicholas Carr / What Hath Tim Berners-Lee Wrought?
Liner Notes flags Nicholas Carr's critique of Tim Berners-Lee's latest book, which pitches the Web as a kind of new human sense — an extension of cognition rather than a disruption of it. The piece is worth a look for how Carr pushes back on that optimism: the existing technological entanglement hasn't exactly enhanced us, and treating further augmentation as obviously good glosses over damage that's already visible. Your note
Stems
AI Tunes Are Flooding Apple Music, and Almost Nobody Is Listening to Them
Stems spots the contradiction buried in Apple Music chief Oliver Schusser's candid comments to Billboard: over a third of uploads are fully AI-generated, yet AI tracks account for well under half a percent of streams — and Apple still won't make AI labeling mandatory. Schusser punts to a hypothetical industry consortium while acknowledging Apple has its own detection tool and that labels are quietly delivering AI music. The piece makes the zero-sum royalty point cleanly: low stream share now doesn't mean low stakes, and voluntary self-tagging is a placeholder, not a policy.
Your note
(Re)Structured News | Tow-Knight Center at CUNY
We have met the enemy, and he is us
The Tow-Knight Center at CUNY ran a conversational AI bot through nearly 40 journalists to ask what's actually blocking AI adoption in newsrooms — and the answer wasn't the tools. It's leadership discomfort, staff anxiety about job loss, and institutional inertia. Heads of audience, public radio product leads, and investigative reporters all describe hitting internal walls, not technical ones. Anyone who's tried to introduce editorial AI workflows inside a label or streaming platform will recognize the pattern immediately.

Building Blocks
A typology of approaches for addressing trend simulation
Brodie Conley's piece reframes trend simulation — AI systems trained to replicate what's already working — as a collective action problem rather than a villain problem. The argument: no single stakeholder is solely to blame, and no single intervention is sufficient. His visual typology maps the toolkit of responses across the full range of parties involved, which is useful precisely because it makes the distributed nature of responsibility harder to dodge. Worth a look for anyone thinking about how platforms, labels, and creators each hold a piece of this.

Sign of the future: GPT-5.5
The most useful observation here isn't about GPT-5.5 specifically — it's about the pattern. Ottessa
Writing Advice #26: How to Get into Someone's Head
Ottessa Moshfegh's Substack column takes up a question most writers quietly dread: how do you write characters whose inner life you haven't lived? Her answer sidesteps the usual craft-manual talk about research and observation — the real move, she argues, is prizing your own vulnerability above all else, because that's the opening through which anything can pass. It's a useful corrective to the idea that cross-gender or cross-experience writing is primarily an empathy exercise.

Finding the Right Emoji
Katherine Needleman uses Michael Tilson Thomas's New York Times obituary as an accidental data point: 38 mentions of professional men, two of women, in a tribute to one of classical music's most celebrated conductors.
