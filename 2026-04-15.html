Vinyl Culture · Music Industry Analysis
They Cloned Her Voice, Then Claimed Her Songs | AI Music Scams Are Using Copyright Law as a Weapon Against Real Artists
Vinyl Culture digs into something uglier than the usual AI-music-is-coming-for-your-job discourse: scammers are now cloning indie artists' voices, uploading AI-generated tracks to streaming platforms, and then weaponizing copyright takedown systems against the original creators. The inversion is almost elegant in its cruelty — the same DMCA infrastructure built to protect artists becomes the tool used to silence them while the clones rack up streams. If you've been wondering what happens when generative audio meets platform-scale content moderation with no human oversight, this is the answer, and it's already happening to artists with no resources to fight back.

No Tags · Music & Scene
San Francisco's techno boom is a warning to the world
No Tags delivers a sharp-edged dispatch from San Francisco's suddenly booming techno scene, and the subtext is a warning dressed as a profile. The same forces that made the city a punchline — tech money, displacement, a hollowed-out creative class — are now funding a nightlife revival that looks suspiciously like content for LinkedIn posts about "community." The piece asks what happens when a scene's growth is underwritten by the industry that killed the last one, and whether the people showing up actually want the culture or just the aesthetic of having been there.

Herb Sundays by Sam Valenti IV · Music & Scene
Us v. Them: The Brooklyn Independent Music Scene of the 00s/10s
Sam Valenti IV's Herb Sundays revisits the Brooklyn indie scene of the 00s and 10s with the kind of granular scene memory that only someone who was there can deliver. This isn't nostalgia — it's an autopsy of how a specific time and place produced a coherent aesthetic identity, and what conditions made that possible before streaming flattened geography and algorithms replaced word-of-mouth. For anyone building or sustaining music communities now, the implicit question is whether those conditions can be reconstructed or whether they were a one-time accident of pre-platform economics.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Vinyl Culture
They Cloned Her Voice, Then Claimed Her Songs | AI Music Scams Are Using Copyright Law as a Weapon Against Real Artists
Vinyl Culture documents a structural exploit, not just a scam: AI actors are using copyright infrastructure as a weapon against the artists it was built to protect. The case of folk musician Murphy Campbell — whose voice was cloned, her songs faked on Spotify, then her original YouTube videos demonetized via Content ID by the same distributor that uploaded the fakes — is the clearest illustration yet of how automation bias creates an inverse property right. The piece also names the conflict nobody's saying out loud: the platform running the automated takedown system is the same company pouring $185 billion into the underlying AI.
