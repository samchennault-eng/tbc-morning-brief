Shhh / Peaceful di Giulio Pecci · Music & Scene
First They Monetized Blackness. Then They Brought Back Guitars.
Shhh / Peaceful traces a familiar arc that anyone watching platform-driven music trends has felt in their bones: the way algorithmic discovery systems first strip-mined Black musical forms for engagement metrics, then pivoted to guitar-driven sounds once the economics shifted. The piece doesn't just document the pattern — it interrogates the machinery underneath, asking what it means when cultural authenticity becomes a variable that platforms optimize for and discard on quarterly cycles. If you've watched a genre go from 'emerging' to 'saturated' to 'legacy' in eighteen months, this offers a structural explanation for that whiplash.

Blood in the Machine · AI + Content & Editorial Technology
A horrifying chart on AI-generated linguistic trends
Blood in the Machine surfaces a chart that should unsettle anyone working in content: the linguistic fingerprints of AI-generated text are now statistically visible at scale, and they're spreading. The piece moves past the usual 'is this AI-written?' parlor game to something more uncomfortable — the way certain phrasings, hedges, and tonal patterns are colonizing written communication whether or not a model actually produced them. For editors and content leads, the implication is stark: the tells aren't just in the slop you're filtering out, they're in the habits you're unconsciously adopting.

Robert Christgau from And It Don't Stop · Music & Scene
The Big Lookback: The Grateful Dead
Christgau's retrospective framing of this 1972 Grateful Dead piece doubles as an intimate origin story—the concert review his future wife Carola first read the morning after they "just about plighted their troth"—and her decades-later description of his prose captures something critics rarely articulate about their own work: "the tone hopped around the way I'd noticed his mind did, first nuts and bolts, then goofy, down-to-earth and sweaty with a touch of, you know, agape." What emerges is less a nostalgic look back at the Dead than a meditation on how personal context shapes textual meaning, how a piece he dismisses as "quality hackwork" produced under deadline pressure became foundational to his marriage. The logistical details ground it beautifully—the busted muffler forcing him onto the Hudson Tubes, the carbon copy carried to Shelter Island, the refusal to cab to a Dead show on Newsday's dime because "it wouldn't have seemed right, somehow." For anyone building archives or resurfacing older work, Christgau demonstrates that curation isn't just about selecting what holds up critically; it's about understanding how context, memoir, and accident collaborate to give writing its lasting charge.

Robert Christgau from And It Don't Stop
The Big Lookback: The Grateful Dead
Robert Christgau resurfaces a 1972 Newsday piece on the Grateful Dead, and the frame around it is as good as the review itself — his then-future wife Carola's description of the writing's tone ('nuts and bolts, then goofy, down-to-earth and sweaty with a touch of agape') is a better encapsulation of what criticism can do than most manifestos. 03 Music Industry Analysis Music
AI Music Newsletter
Deezer says AI-made songs make up 44 percent of daily uploads
AI Music Newsletter flags a Deezer stat that's hard to sit with: 44 percent of daily uploads to the platform are AI-generated, which works out to roughly 75,000 tracks a day. No analysis accompanies the number, but it doesn't need much — that volume alone reframes the catalog inflation problem from a future concern to a present one. For anyone thinking about curation, discovery, or what 'release' even means anymore, this is the baseline.

04 AI + Content & Editorial Technology AI
(Re)Structured News | Tow-Knight Center at CUNY
Who Will Monetize Truth
The Tow-Knight Center at CUNY surfaces a provocative thesis: the sustainable path for media isn't selling stories to general audiences — it's encoding expertise into AI systems and selling intelligence to premium markets. The paper identifies three pressure points: the pivot to niche authority, the encoding of editorial judgment into AI, and an as-yet-nonexistent market for verification and provenance. Anyone watching streaming platforms try to commoditize editorial taste will recognize the structural trap being described here.
