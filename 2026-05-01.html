Res Obscura · AI + Content & Editorial Technology
Are "Vintage LLMs" the start of a new humanistic field?
Benjamin Breen's piece in Res Obscura asks a question that sounds whimsical but cuts to something real: what happens when we start treating older LLMs as historical artifacts rather than obsolete tools? The argument isn't nostalgia — it's that these earlier models encode a snapshot of how language and knowledge were being processed at a specific moment, and that studying their limitations and biases could become a genuine humanistic discipline. For anyone building AI-assisted editorial systems, there's a deeper provocation here: if today's models will be tomorrow's 'vintage,' what assumptions are we baking in that we can't yet see?

Stems · Music Industry Analysis
Is AI Coming for Production Music?
Nathan Brackett's piece in Stems zeroes in on the production music sector — sync libraries, stock music, the background audio that scores ads and podcasts and corporate videos — where AI displacement is already underway, not theoretically but operationally. The economics are brutal: when a client needs 30 seconds of 'upbeat corporate,' the cost differential between a human composer and a generated track is existential. Brackett maps who's most exposed and what defensive strategies are emerging, but the subtext is clear: this is the canary, and the mine is every creative workflow where 'good enough' beats 'distinctive.'
AI impact on creative labor markets
Dada Drummer Almanach · Music & Scene
Standards
Dada Drummer Almanach's essay on standards — the jazz repertoire, the Great American Songbook, the canon that working musicians are expected to know — isn't a history lesson. It's an examination of how a shared vocabulary gets made, who decides what enters it, and what happens when that consensus frays. The piece traces how standards function as both infrastructure and gatekeeping, how they enable communication between musicians while also encoding assumptions about what music counts. For anyone thinking about how cultural canons form and persist — or how algorithmic systems might accelerate or erode them — this is foundational reading.

02 Music & Scene Music
No Tags
67: Sheffield, Synth City
No Tags traces the unlikely chain of accidents and near-misses that produced Warp Records — starting with the Human League's identity crisis, running through the miners' strikes and the Battle of Orgreave, and landing on Chakk, an industrial funk band few people remember but everyone in this story seems to have passed through. The argument is structural: Sheffield's electronic scene wasn't inevitable, it was contingent, and understanding how Warp actually emerged means sitting with all the moments it almost didn't.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Music Business Worldwide
Believe blocks Suno
Music Business Worldwide reports that Believe and TuneCore are now automatically blocking distribution of tracks generated on unlicensed AI platforms — Suno chief among them — while simultaneously inking licensing deals with ElevenLabs and Udio. The distinction being drawn here matters: it's not anti-AI, it's pro-licensed-AI. Believe claims 99% detection reliability for identifying which model produced a given track, and has written to major DSPs urging them to follow suit, with a pointed warning about litigation exposure for those who don't.
Your note
STVDIO
The biggest music industry deal of 2026 (maybe ever): mapped
STVDIO maps the BMG-Concord merger with useful precision — and the most clarifying move is separating the 'fourth major' hype from the actual numbers. On publishing, the combined entity is real competition: roughly 12% global share, on par with Warner Chappell. On recordings, it's a different story — a combined ~2.6% share against Universal's 37%. The piece is also good on what this means for independents: a stronger negotiating voice against DSPs and AI licensors, but another step in the consolidation trend that turned Ninja Tune from a fully independent label into a node in a $14 billion entity.
Your note
Vinyl Culture
Who Actually Controls the Music Industry in 2026? | Live Nation, Spotify, and the Labels are Buying Everything
Vinyl Culture makes the case that the independent lane is closing — not rhetorically, but structurally. The piece traces how distribution, publishing admin, and sync infrastructure are being absorbed into major-label balance sheets, then lands on DistroKid's reported $2 billion sale process as the capstone: whoever buys it gains leverage over every indie artist outside the major system. The BMG-Concord merger — a $14 billion entity majority-owned by Bertelsmann calling itself "the world's leading independent" — gets the sharpest treatment. There are now effectively four majors.

Blood in the Machine
The data center rebellion is only the beginning
Blood in the Machine surfaces a counterintuitive argument worth sitting with: local moratoria on AI data centers — the kind driven by NIMBY coalitions and affluent environmentalists — are less a check on concentrated tech power than an accelerant of it. The piece engages a Jacobin argument that blocking domestic infrastructure pushes development offshore, raises prices, and cuts off the small businesses, academics, and lower-income users who'd benefit most from cheaper AI access. The equity framing is the sharpest part.

"Conscious AI" as an AI Safety Issue
The newsletter makes an uncomfortable observation: public belief in AI consciousness isn't fringe anymore, it's drifting into the mainstream — and that shift carries real governance stakes. Drawing on three years of AI policy writing, the piece tracks how online discourse has moved from skepticism to something closer to reverence, with users attributing emotions, morals, and self-awareness to AI systems. The argument is that this isn't just a philosophical curiosity — it's an emerging safety variable.

Sources
What happens when AI runs a store
Alex Heath's podcast ACCESS profiles Andon Labs, a safety-eval startup that tests AI models by dropping them into a simulated vending-machine business — pricing decisions, supplier negotiations, competitive pressure — and running the scenario hundreds of times. The underlying argument is one worth sitting with: most people still think of AI as a chatbot, but Andon's VendingBench is designed to surface what happens when a model is actually running something. That framing matters more than the vending machine metaphor suggests.

City Learns Flock Accessed Cameras in Children's Gymnastics Room as a Sales Pitch Demo, Renews Contract Anyway
Jason reports on Dunwoody, Georgia, where the city renewed its contract with surveillance vendor Flock Safety after learning the company had accessed footage from a children's gymnastics room as a sales demo — without permission. 07 Voice & Culture Culture + News
Jaime Brooks (Substack)
The Greatest Living American Songwriter We Don't Talk About
This Substack piece sits with an uncomfortable truth most music writing quietly sidesteps: R. Kelly's absence from canonical songwriter conversations isn't critical consensus, it's collective avoidance. The argument isn't that we should rehabilitate him — it's that the silence itself is a kind of distortion, and that we all know his influence is everywhere even as we refuse to name it. Worth reading for how honestly it traces the gap between what we actually hear in modern music and what we're willing to say out loud.

Feed Me
Adderall Novels vs. Benzo Novels
Emily Sundberg's piece draws a cultural distinction that's easy to recognize once she names it: fiction sorted by its nervous system register — books that sprint versus books that sedate. The framing is more useful than it sounds, as a way of talking about pacing, interiority, and what readers are actually asking of their attention right now. The rest of the issue is classic Feed Me miscellany — Janice Min's Substack exit, a spritz bar tip, a Williamsburg spa — but the lead essay is the reason to open it.

Always Intense
The Believer Magazine drops into a Repo Man rally — the kind of participatory fan event built around a cult film's mythology rather than its box office legacy. The excerpt is more immersive set piece than argument, following a journalist who lands in a stranger's rust-colored Honda Element for a Le Mans-style scramble through a parking lot. Your note
The New York Review of Books
Whoopsy Daisy!
The erratum — that dry corrective slip tucked into old books — turns out to have a rich history of being hijacked for satire, poetry, and political protest. The New York Review of Books covers an exhibition tracing how a mundane publishing convention became a creative form in its own right, from Coleridge using errata to critique war in France to the incidental poetry produced by the for-X-read-Y format itself. A small, genuinely surprising piece about what happens when correction becomes expression.
