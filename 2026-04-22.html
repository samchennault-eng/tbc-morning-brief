Contraptions · AI + Content & Editorial Technology
Writing Liveness
Contraptions tackles a question that should haunt anyone working with AI text tools: what makes writing feel alive, and can that quality survive automation? The essay frames 'liveness' not as some mystical authorial presence but as a structural property — the sense that a text is responsive, contingent, written *toward* something rather than assembled from parts. If you've ever read AI-assisted copy and felt something was off without being able to name it, this gives you the vocabulary. The implications for editorial workflows are immediate: efficiency gains mean nothing if the output reads like it was already dead on arrival.

First Floor · Music & Scene
First Floor #308 – Trying Something New
First Floor uses UK grime veteran Mr. Mitch's new release as a lens on artist dissatisfaction with streaming's infinite-shelf logic. Rather than uploading to DSPs, Mitch built a bespoke site where each play degrades the audio file until it eventually dies — currently sitting at 45% integrity. The newsletter pairs this with a Spencer Doran interview about how technology has upended creation and consumption alike. Both cases read as genuine creative resistance to platform defaults, not just stunt-making, and the structural critique lands harder for being embedded in actual practice.

Donny Slater · Music Industry Analysis
Spotify Is the Biggest Store on Earth, But Forgot the Cash Register
Donny Slater lays out a concrete product proposal for Spotify: add an in-app purchase layer that lets indie artists and labels paywall new releases for a window — $20 for early access, 86.5% to the artist and 13.5% to the platform. The 13.5% isn't arbitrary; it sits below Bandcamp's 15%, Apple's small-business rate, and Stripe's fee-plus-percent, making Spotify a payment processor with an already-attached audience rather than a middleman taking a cut of someone else's work. This is an explicit pitch essay and reads like one — short on reporting, long on proposal — but the diagnosis underneath it lands: every handoff to Bandcamp, Shopify, or a link-in-bio kills 60-70% of purchase intent, and Spotify is the only music platform positioned to eliminate that friction entirely. The most useful framing on artist monetization in a while.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Music Business Worldwide
Live Nation and Ticketmaster Lose Antitrust Trial: What Happened, What It Means, and What Comes Next
A federal jury just handed down the biggest structural verdict in live music in decades — finding Live Nation and Ticketmaster guilty on every antitrust claim brought by 33 states, with a determination that consumers were overcharged. Music Business Worldwide walks through the trial's arc and what the remedy phase could mean. That's the part to watch: "what comes next" is genuinely open, and the answer could reshape how tickets are priced, distributed, and controlled across the entire live industry.

Reinhold Niebuhr, then and now
The Brad Mehldau Substack offers something genuinely unusual: a working musician doing serious theological-political reading and writing honestly about what it produces. The hook is Reinhold Niebuhr's 1934 ethics lectures, but the real argument is generational — that Gen-X's particular form of self-awareness, the knowing irony of people whose idealism arrived pre-punctured, might be a genuine virtue rather than a pose. James O'Sullivan's Substack
Why are we whitewashing the allegations against Michael Jackson?
The argument here is structural: celebrity estates can manufacture posthumous innocence by controlling the historical frame. The Michael biopic ends in 1988 — conveniently before every significant abuse allegation — and the piece traces exactly how that choice gets made, from commissioned screenwriters to which family members actually hold veto power. Paris Jackson calling the film full of 'full-blown lies' while confirming her own notes were ignored is the detail that makes the abstraction concrete.
Your note
The Honest Broker
Why Tech Bros Are Watching Videos at 3X Speed
The Honest Broker makes a case worth sitting with: productivity culture is quietly colonizing how we consume art, and the 3x speed podcast habit is the most visible symptom. The argument is that business metrics — efficiency, throughput, ROI on your leisure time — are being imported wholesale into creative experience, with predictable results. It's a framing that should resonate with anyone building editorial products, since the same logic that produces speed-listeners also produces skip-heavy listening sessions.
Your note
Mason Currey
Do bad bosses make for great art?
Mason Currey's Substack uses Jonathan Swift's miserable years under the insufferable Sir William Temple to probe a question anyone with a bad manager will recognize: does creative stagnation actually force the work? Swift couldn't advance politically, fled to a worse situation, came back, and used Temple's library to read the Iliad twice in a year. The argument isn't that suffering is generative — it's that blocked ambition redirects energy somewhere, and that somewhere occasionally turns out to matter.
