Music Business Worldwide · Music Industry Analysis
BMG+Concord is the music industry's biggest bet in years. What's the plan?
Music Business Worldwide breaks down the BMG-Concord merger — the biggest music industry consolidation play in years — and asks the question no one seems to have a clean answer for: what's the actual plan? The piece maps out the catalog math, the debt load, and the strategic logic (or lack thereof) behind creating a third major-adjacent entity in an industry that's already struggling with concentration. If you work anywhere in the label ecosystem or rights landscape, this is essential context for understanding what the next few years of dealmaking might look like.

Patti Smith · Voice & Culture
Happy Birthday Roberto Bolano
Smith's tribute to Roberto Bolaño on what would have been his birthday unfolds as both intimate journal entry and incantatory poem, the former's rambling warmth—failed recordings, restless walks, a fourth reading of *2666*—giving way to the latter's ritualized violence and grief. "Hecatomb" transforms Bolaño's literary universe into a sacrificial altar where oxen become birds become murdered women from the Santa Teresa passages, "a city shaped like a dress / Pierced at the breast / Dripping wands of blood," collapsing the distance between Greek ritual and the brutal femicides at the heart of his work. The poem's structure mirrors the methodical horror it describes: "not butchered all at once / Methodically three-minute intervals," each image detonating with deliberate pacing. What emerges is less criticism than communion, a reminder that the most generative responses to art often refuse the explanatory mode entirely—Smith doesn't analyze Bolaño so much as channel him, creating new work from the friction of readerly devotion.
Literary essay / poetic tribute
Line Noise · Music & Scene
"We carved out that space where we could be anything" - an interview with Coldcut's Matt Black
Cardew's interview with Coldcut's Matt Black surfaces a tension that sits at the heart of creative democratization: the tools that lowered barriers to music-making may have inadvertently flattened the landscape they were meant to enrich. Black is refreshingly candid about this paradox, admitting that despite building software like Jamm to make production "like Legos," he finds himself craving the irreducible weirdness of artists like Thundercat—someone no prompt could conjure. The interview weaves through Coldcut's genre-agnostic catalog, with Black recounting how a five-CD Bollywood compilation sat untouched for years before yielding the sample that became "True Skool," a reminder that serendipity operates on its own timeline. What emerges is less nostalgia than a working philosophy: that curatorial identity—Coldcut "carved out that space where we could be anything"—matters more than format or BPM. For anyone building recommendation systems or thinking through AI-assisted creative workflows, Black's ambivalence offers a useful corrective: ease of production is not the same as resonance, and the human capacity for unlikely juxtaposition remains the thing worth protecting.

02 Music & Scene Music
All Things Jazz
Charles Mingus Would Not Behave: Mingus vs. the Establishment
All Things Jazz makes a structural argument that most Mingus profiles miss: his refusal to call himself a jazz musician wasn't branding contrarianism, it was a diagnosis. The genre label organized Black musical expression in ways that made it marketable without making it respectable. The piece traces how that philosophical position cashed out practically — co-founding Debut Records with Max Roach in 1952, decades before artist ownership became a talking point — and eventually politically, through "Fables of Faubus." The throughline is consistent: categorization as containment.

Look What You Made Me Do
In Defense of...Greg Ginn?
Eugene S. Robinson's piece uses his own band's SST Records experience as a lens for reassessing Greg Ginn — the famously shady architect of American hardcore's most important label. The argument isn't that Ginn was misunderstood, exactly, but that the full picture resists the villain-only read. Robinson moves through Black Flag's history with the authority of someone who was actually there — from Irving Plaza to the Mabuhay — and the contractual wariness makes the portrait more credible, not less.

Friday Roundup: Examining Drake's 'ICEMAN' Installation
The andre gee newsletter uses Drake's ICEMAN installation — an ice block stunt that ended with a streamer pocketing $50K and an album date reveal that felt pre-arranged — as a jumping-off point for a wider look at physical, fan-facing rollouts. Donny Slater
Can Getting Blackballed Be a Blessing?
The more interesting argument here isn't about blackballing itself — it's about what the fear of it does to everyone who never gets blackballed. This Substack piece makes the case that pre-emptive self-censorship is the real culture killer: artists and execs quietly water down their positions before anyone asks, trading conviction for access until the whole industry goes politely stale. The actual exile, the piece argues, at least forces clarity.
