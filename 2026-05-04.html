Read Max · AI + Content & Editorial Technology
People prefer A.I. art because people prefer bad art
Read Max takes on one of the more uncomfortable findings floating around AI discourse — that in blind tests, people often prefer AI-generated art — and refuses the easy dismissals. The argument isn't that taste is broken or that audiences are philistines; it's that the aesthetic middle has always dominated, and AI is exceptionally good at producing competent, inoffensive, familiar work. The piece lands somewhere unsettling: if most people preferred mediocrity before AI, the technology just makes that preference cheaper to serve. For anyone building editorial products or arguing for craft, this is the counterargument you need to actually engage.

Bruce Ramos · Music Industry Analysis
AI Music Just Got Its First PRO
Bruce Ramos examines a quiet but structurally significant development: AI-generated music now has its own performing rights organization. The piece doesn't treat this as a curiosity — it traces the implications for how royalties flow, who collects them, and what happens when algorithmic compositions start claiming the same mechanical rights as human ones. The real tension is whether existing PROs will recognize these entities or whether a parallel rights infrastructure is emerging alongside the traditional one. If you're tracking how AI reshapes music's economic plumbing, this is where the pipes are being laid.

Evil Speakers · Music & Scene
The Losers' History of The Black Keys
Evil Speakers delivers a career reassessment of The Black Keys that's less interested in what they achieved than in how they became the wrong band at the wrong time — too raw for mainstream acceptance when they started, too polished to stay credible when they broke through, and ultimately trapped by a rock revival that never quite revived. The piece works as music criticism, but it's really about the structural forces that make certain careers feel like near-misses even when the numbers say otherwise. Anyone who's watched an artist navigate the gap between critical positioning and commercial reality will recognize the pattern.

02 Music & Scene Music
Flagging Down the Double E's
One of the Most Talked-About Shows of the Rolling Thunder Revue
Ray Padgett reconstructs the New Orleans leg of Dylan's 1976 Rolling Thunder Revue through the people who were actually there — and the picture that emerges is less mythic road show, more chaotic collision of egos. The anchor story is Tom Waits getting his own show hijacked by McGuinn, Baez, and Kinky Friedman while Dylan watched from the front row. Padgett's sourcing — soundmen, bandleaders, a widow — gives the piece a texture that pure Dylan lore usually flattens.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Music Business Worldwide
3 Observations On… BMG x Concord
Music Business Worldwide breaks down the structural logic of the BMG-Concord merger — and the numbers the companies conspicuously didn't disclose. The deal creates a Bertelsmann-majority entity (67%) that sits above both Concord Records and BMG Publishing, with Concord's Bob Valentine stepping into the CEO role. The piece is most useful for what it reads into the financial breadcrumbs the announcement did offer, giving a clearer picture of scale and strategic intent than the official release.

Bruce Ramos
The Record Deal Is Dying. Four Catalog Buyers Just Got Built.
The Substack argues that the death of the traditional label deal and the rise of catalog buyers are the same story told from opposite sides of the table — and most operators are still reading only one side. The piece is structured as a five-step action list for label, distributor, and catalog operators: audit for buyer-readiness (not just creative health), map the buyer landscape from Concord to Duetti, pick a strategic position, tighten IP terms in new artist deals, and consider whether your genre vertical is an acquirable vehicle before someone else builds it first.

the andre gee newsletter
Friday Roundup: Bill Ackman Buying UMG Would Be Disastrous For Free Expression
The andre gee newsletter makes the case that a Bill Ackman acquisition of UMG isn't just a business story — it's a free expression story. The argument is straightforward: Ackman's well-documented political positions make him a uniquely legible threat compared to the billionaire label owners we already have. With a third of the world's recorded music — including hip-hop — potentially under his control, the piece asks what happens to artists whose politics don't align. No clean answer, but the question feels worth sitting with.

04 AI + Content & Editorial Technology AI
Contraptions
Getting Gooier
Contraptions makes an uncomfortable argument most AI discourse quietly avoids: everyone's modeling how the world changes under AI, but almost nobody is seriously examining how humans change. The piece calls out the lazy shorthand — 'generalists will thrive,' 'storytellers will be valued,' 'lawyers will disappear' — as a way of pretending human nature stays fixed while everything else shifts. Worth reading for anyone building content strategy on assumptions about what audiences will want from humans versus machines.
Your note
(Re)Structured News | Tow-Knight Center at CUNY
Trust Busters
The Tow-Knight Center's (Re)Structured News raises a question that doesn't get enough airtime: if half of internet traffic is already machine-generated, what does trust even mean when the audience is mostly bots talking to each other? The signals humans use — brand names, bylines, social proof — may not translate to agentic AI systems, and the infrastructure for machine-readable credentialing barely exists yet. For anyone whose value proposition depends on being a trusted source, that's a structural problem worth thinking about now.

David by Design
Domain is the New Data
David by Design makes the case that AI's hunger has moved past behavioral data into something more specific: elite human cognition. The argument is that we're in a 'domain grab' — OpenAI paying specialists up to $500/hour, Scale AI and Invisible Technologies quietly building RLHF pipelines staffed by doctors, lawyers, and coders — and that the experts being hired are, in effect, training their own replacements. Worth sitting with for anyone whose work trades on specialized knowledge.

The Believer Magazine
Negative Utopia: Tolerating Abstraction
Lucy Ives's final installment of her Guggenheim-recognized column for *The Believer* zeroes in on a specific and underreported failure mode of LLMs: that AI-generated citations are too perfect to be real — hallucinated sources that match your thesis so neatly they read as satire. The 40% hallucination rate from research librarians is the kind of concrete figure that cuts through the hype, and Ives uses it to interrogate what it means to 'tolerate abstraction' in AI-assisted intellectual work.

The Crimes Unforgivable
Look What You Made Me Do opens with a disarmingly simple premise — a train ride, a friendly-looking stranger — and uses it to explore something harder to name: the way certain people become magnets for confessions they never asked for. Ottessa Moshfegh Substack
Assignment: THE FICTIONAL BIOGRAPHY
Ottessa's piece reframes the perennial 'how do you build a believable character' question by rejecting the premise — don't research real people, invent fake historical ones. The argument is that writing mini-biographies of imagined figures from the past builds narrative authority precisely because you can't second-guess yourself. It's a craft-focused Substack post rather than a reported piece, but the underlying logic — that false documents are honest about their subjectivity in a way 'true' ones aren't — is worth sitting with.

The Culturist
Why Beauty Is a Gateway to the Divine
The Culturist makes a counterintuitive case: the decadent movement and Christianity aren't opposites — they're two responses to the same underlying hunger. Oscar Wilde's deathbed conversion is the entry point, but the piece uses a pattern (Beardsley, Huysmans, and others made the same turn) to argue something structural about aestheticism itself. If you take beauty seriously enough, the argument goes, you end up somewhere transcendent. Interesting framing for anyone thinking about how culture moves people toward meaning.
