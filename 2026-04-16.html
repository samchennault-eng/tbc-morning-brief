Herb Sundays by Sam Valenti IV · Music & Scene
Us v. Them: The Brooklyn Independent Music Scene of the 00s/10s
Sam Valenti IV's Herb Sundays has been quietly assembling one of the better oral histories of the Brooklyn indie scene's peak years, and this installment lands somewhere between elegy and autopsy. The piece traces how a specific cluster of venues, labels, and social networks created the conditions for a genuinely weird moment in American guitar music — and how the usual suspects (rising rents, platform attention economics, the professionalization of DIY) hollowed it out. Valenti writes from inside the room, which gives the nostalgia some teeth. Worth reading less as history than as a case study in how scenes actually die.

All Things Jazz · Music & Scene
Billie Holiday: What She Sang Anyway
All Things Jazz returns to Billie Holiday not with biography but with a close listen to what she actually did with her voice — the micro-timing, the way she sat behind or ahead of the beat, how she bent lyrics into something more spoken than sung. The piece argues that Holiday's influence gets flattened into "emotional delivery" when the real innovation was structural: she treated the standard songbook as raw material to be dismantled and rebuilt in real time. For anyone thinking about how interpretation becomes authorship, this is the tradition.

Fluxblog: Music Blog · Music & Scene
Fluxblog 600: Early Days of the Global Pop Underground
Fluxblog hits its 600th edition with a retrospective on the early MP3 blog era — when a handful of writers with Blogspot accounts and decent taste were genuinely shaping what got heard. Matthew Perpetua's reflection isn't just nostalgia; it's a useful artifact of what discovery looked like before algorithmic recommendation colonized the entire experience. The piece surfaces how fragile and personal that infrastructure was, and how quickly it gave way to systems that could scale. Anyone building curation products now should understand what got lost in the transition.

Herb Sundays by Sam Valenti IV
Us v. Them: The Brooklyn Independent Music Scene of the 00s/10s
Sam Valenti IV — founder of Ghostly International, so someone who lived this — writes about Ronen Givony's new book on the Brooklyn indie scene of the 00s/10s, and the framing is usefully unsentimental: the scene was a collective delusion, potent precisely because everyone believed in it at once. Givony tracks how larger political and economic forces enabled the moment and then snapped it back, leaving 'indie' as a marketing term rather than a culture. Worth reading alongside anyone thinking about what scenes actually do before they get legible to the industry.

All Things Jazz
Billie Holiday: What She Sang Anyway
All Things Jazz argues that Holiday's persecution wasn't incidental to her art — it was a direct response to it. The piece frames 'Strange Fruit' not as protest music but as prosecution, and traces how the state's campaign against her (Anslinger's FBN, the revoked cabaret card, the Lexington facility) was designed to banish rather than punish. It earns weight by drawing the throughline to the opioid crisis: the double standard isn't subtle, and the piece doesn't let it be.

Fluxblog: Music Blog
Fluxblog 600: Early Days of the Global Pop Underground
Matthew Perpetua's Fluxblog — one of the original MP3 blogs, now hitting issue 600 — uses a milestone playlist as a way to revisit a pre-algorithmic moment when global pop connoisseurship was genuinely underground. The framing is personal (early 20s, alternate universe) but the observation is real: the infrastructure for 'alternative pop for snobby connoisseurs' didn't exist yet, and these songs had nowhere to land. Artists like Robyn, M.I.A., and The Knife eventually broke through; most on this list didn't.

Herbie Hancock: Inventions & Dimensions
All Things Jazz makes a structural argument about *Inventions & Dimensions* that goes beyond anniversary appreciation: the album works because Hancock placed rhythm at the absolute center and let harmony and melody emerge from it, rather than the reverse. The piece situates that choice in its moment — the early-'60s fracturing of jazz orthodoxy that gave a conceptual thinker room to move. Track-by-track analysis grounds the argument in specifics, though the piece cuts off before finishing.
