First Floor · Music & Scene
Music Discourse Is Plentiful, Often Angry and Increasingly Not About Music at All
First Floor takes on the peculiar state of music discourse in 2025, where the most heated conversations about music seem to be about everything except the music itself. The piece traces how platform incentives have warped discussion toward identity positioning, parasocial attachment, and context-collapse controversies—the kind of engagement that algorithms reward but that leaves actual sonic or artistic consideration as an afterthought. If you've noticed your timeline getting louder about artists while getting quieter about albums, this essay names the structural forces behind that drift.
Cultural criticism (identity/politics in music)
DADA DRUMMER ALMANACH · Music Industry Analysis
Music Streaming is a Prediction Market
DADA DRUMMER ALMANACH offers a genuinely novel frame for understanding streaming economics: music platforms as prediction markets, where every catalog acquisition and playlist placement is essentially a bet on future attention. The argument cuts through the usual royalty-rate discourse to examine what happens when the entire business model is built on forecasting listener behavior rather than serving it. For anyone watching the recent flurry of catalog deals and wondering what the endgame looks like, this reframing makes the logic—and its potential collapse points—considerably clearer.
Platform strategy pivot
Joel Gouveia's Substack · Music Industry Analysis
The Music Industry is Dying. So Why Is Wall Street Buying?
Joel Gouveia's Substack digs into a genuinely puzzling contradiction: streaming revenues are flattening, artist economics remain brutal, and yet Wall Street keeps pouring money into music assets like they're buying gold. The piece unpacks the financial logic—catalog as yield-bearing asset, music as inflation hedge, rights as the new real estate—and asks whether this capital influx is actually building anything or just extracting value from a system that can't generate enough for the people who make the work. A sharp read for anyone trying to reconcile the industry's triumphalist headlines with the reality on the ground.

02 Music & Scene Music
Purposeful Listening
On Learning the Santur and Rethinking Classical Music Ideals
Purposeful Listening runs a candid account of learning the santur that turns into something sharper: a critique of how classical traditions — Iranian, Western, all of them — enforce a singular ideal of correct sound, and how that enforcement lands hardest on people caught between cultures. The piece is most interesting when it connects physical rebellion against the instrument (prepared strings, paper clips, deliberate damage) to the larger argument that mastery defined by perfection forecloses the music that comes from community, exploration, and what you actually want to say.

Cuba on Record
Los Saicos: Peru's Punk Primal Scream. An Interview With Erwin Flores.
Judy Cantor-Navas uses Munster Records' reissue of Los Saicos' complete recordings as a hook to revisit the Peruvian band's improbable second life — and the ongoing argument about whether punk actually started in Lima in 1965. The piece pairs reissue news with an interview Cantor-Navas conducted for her podcast *Punk in Translation*, and frontman Erwin Flores turns out to be genuinely compelling: crowd-surfing in Madrid at 70-something, claiming he growled because he couldn't actually sing, and apparently spending the intervening decades working for NASA.

Line Noise
"Humbleness is important" - an interview with Olof Dreijer
Ben Cardew's interview with Olof Dreijer (one half of The Knife) turns on a quietly sharp observation: that refusing to center individual faces in your live show gets read as mystique, not generosity, because the music industry's default grammar is the solo protagonist. Dreijer's debut album under their own name is partly a response to that — stepping forward, owning privilege, dropping the Berlin-drag ambiguity of earlier projects in favor of warmth and directness. Worth reading for the candor.

03 Music Industry Analysis Music
Money 4 Nothing
Wild Geese Chase and a $64 billion offer for UMG
Money 4 Nothing takes a darkly comic look at two stories that are really the same story: Bill Ackman's $64 billion bid for UMG (a hedge fund manager who thinks the music industry's problem is insufficient shareholder returns) and the Geese astroturfing scandal (a band that hired a firm to simulate organic buzz). Both hinge on the same anxiety — that authentic culture is already a managed simulation, and we just prefer not to notice. The UMG analysis is the sharper half, examining what financialization actually means for an industry already structured around extraction.

Music Business Worldwide
Suno is Now the 'Controller' of Songkick User Data – and is Hiring a GM to Wire it into Its AI Music Platform
Music Business Worldwide reports that Suno has formally taken control of Songkick's user data — artist preferences, location info, alert settings — five months after acquiring the concert discovery platform from Warner Music Group. The data transfer is the clearest signal yet that Suno sees live-music intent signals as training or personalization fuel, not just a product acquisition. An open GM hire to integrate Songkick into the AI platform confirms this isn't passive — they're actively wiring real listener behavior into whatever comes next.

Zinstrel - AI Music Culture & Analysis
The Great AI Music Filtering Has Begun
The same week TuneCore started auto-blocking tracks from unlicensed AI platforms like Suno, Spotify rolled out verified badges to flag human artists — and Zinstrel argues the timing isn't coincidence. It's infrastructure-level coordination. Believe's CEO is claiming 99% detection accuracy and has already written to major DSPs asking them to adopt the same tools. The emerging framework is essentially: licensing deal gets you in, litigation gets you blocked. Worth watching for how this reshapes which AI music tools survive.

When stories become liquid
The Media Copilot takes on "liquid content" — the idea that AI makes any story infinitely remixable into any format on demand. Music Business Worldwide
France Urges Lawmakers to Pass Bill Requiring AI Firms to Prove They Didn't Use Copyrighted Content
Music Business Worldwide covers a significant shift in how copyright liability could work in AI cases: France's proposed Darcos law would flip the burden of proof, requiring AI companies to demonstrate they didn't use copyrighted material rather than forcing rights holders to prove they did. A coalition of 81 cultural and media organizations — music, film, publishing, press — is now pressing the National Assembly to schedule a vote. If it passes, it's the kind of structural lever that could reshape how AI training deals get negotiated everywhere.

06 AI Product News AI
404 Media
The AI Hard Drive Shortage Is Making It More Expensive and Harder to Archive the Internet
404 Media reports that the AI infrastructure boom is hitting archiving projects from two directions at once: hardware costs have spiked as data centers hoover up SSDs and RAM, and anti-scraping countermeasures aimed at AI bots are accidentally locking out the Internet Archive too. The End of Term Archive — which preserves government websites between administrations — had to scale back planned server upgrades because the prices were simply too high. The EFF's framing is sharp: blocking the Archive doesn't stop AI training, it just erases the historical record.

Lenny's Newsletter
Why SaaS freemium playbooks don't work in AI, and what to do instead
Vikas Kansal's piece for Lenny's Newsletter names a structural problem anyone building on AI is running into: the freemium model that made SaaS growth so clean doesn't port over, because every free user interaction burns real compute. Kansal leads product for Google AI — Gemini, NotebookLM, Veo3 — so he's working at a scale where this tension is unavoidable. The piece draws on that experience to lay out what actually works when paywalling AI products without killing the growth loop.

The VC Corner
Model-Market Fit: The New Make-or-Break for AI Startups
The VC Corner argues that product-market fit and founder-market fit both skip a step that's specific to AI: whether the model can actually do what the market needs, and whether the business model aligns with how that market buys. The piece splits this into two distinct questions — technical fit (can it perform without constant correction?) and commercial fit (does pricing and packaging match how customers actually spend?). A useful frame for evaluating any AI vendor pitch.

07 Voice & Culture Culture + News
The New York Review of Books
War Breeds Tyrants
The New York Review of Books opens with a sharp structural argument: Western intervention doesn't just destabilize governments, it kills the cultural conditions that make democracy possible. The 1953 CIA-MI6 coup against Iran's Mossadegh is the entry point — a reminder that the Shah's dictatorship wasn't an accident but a design. The piece uses Mehdi Akhavan-Sales's poem 'Winter' to trace how political defeat seeps into art and erodes collective spirit. Useful historical grounding for anyone thinking about how power shapes culture.
Your note
Katherine Needleman Oboist's Substack
TO THE COLLEGE KIDS SITTING IN FRONT OF ME
Katherine Needleman's piece starts as a quiet evening — a good dinner, students playing well — and then turns on the particular cruelty of being mocked in public by people who don't realize you're sitting right there. Oboist and teacher Needleman describes watching college students at her students' own recital scroll through her photos and snicker, apparently never considering she might be in the room. It's a sharp, first-person account of what parasocial contempt looks like from the other side of the screen.
Your note
Mason Currey
Get the Money!
Mason Currey uses Helen DeWitt's decision to turn down a Windham-Campbell Prize over its promotional obligations as a jumping-off point for the argument that runs through all three of his books: the artists who refuse to perform a public self are often the ones most capable of making something lasting. It's a compact, recognizable observation — the same tension any serious creative navigates — and Currey lands it cleanly by grounding it in a concrete, recent example rather than abstract principle.

Gary's Journey Through Hell
The Case Against Mondays
Gary Shteyngart's case against Mondays is exactly what it sounds like: a short, personal argument for the four-day work week, grounded in the ritual dread of Sunday nights and the grim transition back to the laptop. The piece is more comic confession than policy analysis — Shteyngart admits he works five days anyway — but the core proposal is earnest enough. If you've ever eaten too much on a Sunday to cope with what's coming, you'll recognize the feeling he's describing.
